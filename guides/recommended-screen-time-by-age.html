AAP guidelines by age — 2026

Age Recommended daily limit Notes
Under 18 months None Video chat with family is the only exception
18–24 months Minimal High-quality programming only, co-viewed with a parent
2–5 years 1 hour/day High-quality content; co-view and discuss when possible
6–8 years 1–2 hours/day Consistent limits; screens shouldn't displace sleep or play
9–12 years 1–2 hours recreational School days vs. weekends may differ; homework use is separate
13+ years Family guidelines No fixed limit; ensure sleep (8–10 hrs), activity (1 hr/day) aren't displaced

The most important thing the AAP says isn't a specific hour count — it's that screen time shouldn't displace four essentials: adequate sleep, physical activity (1 hour daily for ages 6+), homework and learning, and face-to-face family time. Use those four as your real test.

What the research actually shows

Kids ages 8-12 spend an average of 5-6 hours on screens daily — well above AAP guidelines. Teens clock up to 9 hours. The gap between recommended and actual isn't because parents don't care. It's because enforcement is hard without a system.

The type of content matters as much as the duration. The AAP distinguishes between passive consumption (scrolling, YouTube), interactive use (video chat, educational apps), and educational programming. The same 90 minutes looks very different if it's TikTok versus learning to code.

Gaming specifically gets more nuance in recent research. Studies show gaming can support problem-solving, coordination, and even social skills — the concern is displacement of other activities, not the gaming itself.

How to actually enforce the limits

Knowing the guidelines is the easy part. Enforcing them daily without constant conflict is where most families struggle. A few approaches that work:

The earn model: Screen time starts at zero and must be earned through tasks. This aligns daily limits with natural effort — kids who do more earn more, up to your daily cap. It also eliminates arguments because the system decides, not you.

Fixed time windows: Screen time is allowed only between specific hours (e.g., 4-6pm on school days). Simple to understand, easy to enforce, requires no daily negotiation.

Built-in platform controls: Apple Screen Time, Google Family Link, and Nintendo's parental controls all offer daily time limits. The limitation is they don't incentivize — they just cut off access, which often triggers the argument anyway.

Frequently asked questions

How much screen time should a 5 year old have?
The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends no more than 1 hour per day of high-quality programming for children ages 2-5. For a 5-year-old, 1 hour of screen time per day is the guideline, with content co-viewed with a parent when possible.
How much screen time should an 8 year old have?
For children ages 6 and older, the AAP doesn't set a specific daily limit but recommends that screen time not displace sleep, physical activity, homework, or face-to-face interaction. A practical guideline for an 8-year-old is 1-2 hours of recreational screen time per day on school days and up to 3 hours on weekends.
How much screen time should a 10 year old have?
For a 10-year-old, the AAP recommends consistent limits that don't displace sleep (9-12 hours), physical activity (1 hour daily), and homework. Most pediatricians suggest 1-2 hours of recreational screen time on school days. The type of content matters as much as the amount — interactive and educational use is viewed more favorably than passive consumption.
Is 3 hours of screen time too much for a child?
For school-age children on a school day, 3 hours of recreational screen time is above most pediatric guidelines. On weekends or school holidays, 2-3 hours is more commonly accepted. The real test: are sleep, physical activity, and homework being displaced? If not, the total hours matter less than the context.
What is the AAP screen time recommendation for 2026?
The AAP recommends: no screen time under 18 months except video chat; 18-24 months: high-quality programming only, co-viewed with parents; ages 2-5: limit to 1 hour per day; ages 6+: consistent limits ensuring screens don't displace sleep, physical activity, or homework. The AAP Family Media Plan tool helps families create personalized guidelines.
